THE ART OF THE EXIT
Everyone obsesses over the entry. The hardest part of the trade — and the part that actually decides your ultimate fate and longevity in the space — is how you get out.

YOUR CREDIBLE EXIT
A "credible exit" isn't a universal rule you copy from someone else. It's the one you'll actually honor — because it fits how you're wired and what you're trying to achieve.
DEFINE IT BEFORE YOU NEED IT
A CREDIBLE EXIT IS A DECISION YOU ALREADY MADE.
1 Pre-committed. Chosen while you're calm and objective — not mid-trade with money on the line.
2 Specific. A condition you can actually see hit — not "I'll know it when I feel it."
3 Yours. Sized to your personality and goal — so you'll honor it under pressure.
In the moment, your brain is the least reliable it will ever be. The whole point of a credible exit is to move the decision to a time when you can think clearly — and then just follow it.
Scenario planning beats willpower. You're not trying to be disciplined in the heat of it. You're trying to not need to be.

AT SOME POINT, YOU WIN ON PURPOSE
THINK BIG — THEN CLAIM VICTORY.
CLAIM IT
the dream you don't need
Optimism is an asset — keep it. But a position you never close is just a feeling, not a result. At some point you have to convert the move into a realized win and respect what the downside could take back.
Claiming victory is a skill. The chart doesn't ring a bell at the top — you decide what "enough" means, in advance, and you take it.
THE EXIT THAT FITS YOU
WHICH MISTAKE IS YOURS?
GIVES IT BACK
great at finding winners · holds too long
SELLS TOO SOON
books wins fast · caps the upside
Two traders, opposite leaks. The right exit rule for one would wreck the other. So the first move isn't a chart — it's an honest read of which one you actually are.
PROFILE A
YOU TAKE WINNERS WELL — BUT YOU GIVE THEM BACK.
THE LEAK HOLDS TOO LONG
✓ Great at spotting setups and finding the next opportunity
✕ Rides winners past the top and hands back too much
→ The fix: get out at your spots and claim the win. Trust that you'll find more.
If finding trades is your superpower, you can afford to leave some of the max upside on the table. Trading a little ceiling for clean, repeatable wins is a great deal for you.
Because giving it back doesn't just cost money — it costs confidence and momentum , the two things your edge actually runs on. Protect those and the next setup is right there.
PROFILE B
YOU LOCK WINS FAST — BUT YOU SELL TOO SOON.
THE LEAK EXITS EARLY
✓ Disciplined, banks gains, rarely gives much back
✕ Snips winners early — never lets a big one fully run
→ The fix: practice holding for the bigger winner — let a runner run.
Your discipline is real — but it's capping you. A few big winners held to target can change your whole curve more than a stack of small, early exits ever will.
This is how you scale as a trader: learn to sit through the noise on your best ideas, and let size plus patience do the compounding.
THE REAL PRICE OF A BAD EXIT
IT'S NOT JUST THE P&L. IT'S THE CONFIDENCE.
GIVES IT BACK · CONFIDENCE & MOMENTUM
drains every time you hand a winner back
tilt · hesitation · smaller next size
SELLS TOO SOON · YOUR CEILING
the upside you never let compound
small, early wins · a ceiling you keep capping
Pick the exit style that protects the asset your edge actually runs on. For one trader that's momentum and conviction; for the other it's the size of their winners. Optimize for your future self, not the perfect trade.
YOU DON'T HAVE TO CHOOSE
SCALE OUT — CLAIM THE WIN AND KEEP A RUNNER.

Selling in pieces is a psychological tool, not a compromise. Booking part of the position satisfies the urge to claim victory — which quiets the brain that wants to bail on the whole thing.
And the runner you leave on keeps you exposed to the big outcome. It lets each trader lean against their own leak — the giver-backer banks more sooner, the early-seller stays in longer.
THE TAKEAWAY
The entry is a guess. The exit is where you show what you know about yourself.
Map your credible exits while you're calm. Set the guardrails. Trust the process over the outcome. And build the exit that fits the trader you actually are — not the one you wish you were at the top of a chart.
