                                         --- Quick Aside ---
I tried to answer this question somewhat, but ultimately stumbled on some strange data that
probably could use more investigating. Nonetheless, I felt like I would share the results for you
all!
Let’s suppose there was a liquidity drain. How could we measure it, and what should we expect
to find?
Perhaps the first consequence of a sudden collapse in liquidity would be large price
movements; since poor liquidity means one-of-the-sides-of-the-book aren’t playing well with the
other, causing one of the sides-of-the-book to have to step over a large gap to transact, and
thus, cause greater moves in price (this is the same principal as larger IV values foretell large
moves in price but a bit more nuanced since LDPM-Vol measures liquidity directly).


Deep Dive Stocks – Weekly Chat                                                         10/09/2023
So since there might be a connection with the change in price and liquidity, let’s set up a quick
ratio to measure, well, the change in price as compared to the change in liquidity!
To do this, I took the %-daily change in LDPM-Vol and the %-daily change in $SPY’s price and
computed:
                                                                  ΔLDPMvol
                                                   𝑅𝑅𝑅𝑅𝑅𝑅𝑅𝑅𝑅𝑅 =
                                                                   Δ𝑃𝑃𝑃𝑃𝑃𝑃𝑃𝑃𝑃𝑃
This should show us how much LDPM-Vol it takes to move the price 1%. The greater the value,
the less liquidity there is, and the lower the value, the more liquidity there is1:




The graph shows a relatively benign behavioral set except for a few larger-than-normal spikes in
behavior2. Interestingly, these spikes occur at-or-around larger downturns in price.
Let’s map out these values directly. First let’s establish a base-line for $SPY with regards to the
Ratio:



1

means when the ratio increases, liquidity decreases.

        These results are somewhat confusing! Whereas we can see that the “LDPM-Vol Spike” seems
        to be related in some-way to changes in price-action (with the running hypothesis that these
        spikes demonstrate times when sudden collapses of liquidity occur, and as such, things
        reverse), it would seem that post-these liquidity collapses, liquidity on average improves.
        We can see this with the average daily Ratio calculations which show an average of 21.75 – a
        120% decrease from the average daily LDPM-Vol ratio as compared to $SPY as a whole. That’s
        a lot of liquidity! (Remember: a decrease in ratio → an increase in liquidity).
        Initially this was confusing: if the premise was that these spikes represent sudden loses of
        liquidity – why is it that post-spike the liquidity situation improves ? Well – it might be that its
        because in these moments are defined by poor liquidity leading up to, and return of liquidity on
        the consequent !
